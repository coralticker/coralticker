"What does that cost?" is the question every named coral dodges. A name like Homewrecker or Walt Disney isn't a price — it's a range, and a wide one. The same name can be a small frag at one shop and a grown-out colony at another in the same week, and neither number is wrong. So this is a map of what reefers actually pay for the named corals they chase right now, across the whole spread — zoas to acros, mushrooms to torches — and why the number you should trust is never a single number.

Every price below is live: the lowest in-stock listing across the vendors I check, pulled the moment you load this page, not a figure I typed in once and forgot. Named corals move, and stock comes and goes, so if a name here isn't listed anywhere right now, the page says so instead of showing you a dead number. There's a lot of ground between the cheapest and the dearest, so I've grouped them by roughly what they go for — cheapest first.

## Where named corals start.

You don't need grail money to own a coral with a name and a story. The floor of the named market is zoas sold by the polyp and a few hardy starter pieces — recognizable lineages that happen not to have caught a four-figure fever.

Strawberry Shortcake is the cautionary one here. The name got so popular that several different corals now wear it, grown by different shops, and the price reflects the grower as much as the coral. A cheap "Strawberry Shortcake" might be a real deal or might be a frag that has no business carrying the name — which is exactly the kind of gap a price on its own can't close.

The torch and the bounces tell you something the acros don't: the most expensive named corals aren't always acros. A single torch head or one bounce mushroom can outprice a colony of almost anything above, because rarity and how slowly the thing grows matter more than the genus.

## Why one price tells you nothing.

Here's the part the listicles skip. A named coral's price isn't a fact you can look up — it's a moving target, and a single sticker is the least useful version of it.

The same name lists under slightly different prices at different shops in the same week. It swings with frag size, with how recently a shop fragged the mother, with whether two people who both "have to have it" happened to be online when it dropped. A low price might be a genuine break or might be a renamed nobody. The number only means something next to its history — what the name has actually gone for, across shops, over weeks.

That's what the price-history pages are for. Take [JF Homewrecker](/coral/jf-homewrecker/price-history), a name that swings hard from listing to listing — the history tells you whether today's number is a real deal or just an ordinary Tuesday. Same with [Walt Disney](/coral/wwc-walt-disney-acropora/price-history): when it does turn up, the only way to know if the asking price is sane is to see where it's been.

So use the live numbers above as a gut-check on the name, not as a target.
