The best corals don't wait for you. A grail acro — the rare, named stuff reefers chase — lists at nine in the morning, and by the time you've read the name and reached for your card, someone else has already checked out. Lose that race enough times and rare corals start to feel impossible to buy on purpose — like they only go to people with some kind of inside track.

There is an inside track. It's just that nobody writes it down. It's the stuff you pick up after years of losing live sales, getting burned on a renamed frag, and watching a $400 colony arrive dead in the bag. This is what I've worked out watching these corals move across the shops I check every day — the mechanics first, the shortcuts second.

This guide runs in two halves: **find it and win it**, then **buy smart and keep it alive**.

## Part A — find it and win it.

Speed and access decide most of this half. The corals you're chasing sell through four channels, and each one rewards a different kind of fast:

| Channel | How fast you need to be | How you actually win |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed drop | Seconds — first to *complete* checkout wins | Account and one-tap pay set up before it lists |
| Live sale | Seconds, over and over, on dozens of listings | The same prep, plus knowing the lineup is coming |
| Auction | Slow until the final minute, then all at once | A ceiling set before it opens — bid it once, or walk |
| Waffle / raffle | No speed, but a cap on entries | An entry before the cap fills; the draw is luck |

## The cart doesn't reserve it.

**Adding a coral to your cart holds nothing.** On most shops the coral is yours when your order is *complete*, not when it's sitting in the cart. In a live sale — dozens of corals posted over half an hour, a crowd all hitting the same listings — the winner is whoever finishes checkout first, and it's usually decided in seconds.

So the whole game is checkout speed, set up before the sale starts:

1. **Make your account ahead of time,** with shipping address and card already saved. Guest checkout loses.
2. **Turn on one-tap pay** — ShopPay, Apple Pay, whatever the shop supports. A saved one-tap beats typing a card number every single time.
3. **Pay by credit card, not the PayPal redirect.** That extra hop is exactly where the coral slips away.
4. **Check whether you have to add a heat pack or shipping module yourself.** Some shops won't ship livestock without one, and some require it on orders under a certain total — miss it and your order can get held or cancelled after you thought you'd won.

None of this is cheating. It's just removing the ten seconds of fumbling that the person who beat you had already removed.

## Every shop has a rhythm.

**Drops aren't random, even though they feel that way when you're refreshing.** Most shops settle into a cadence — a ship day or two each week (often midweek, so livestock isn't sitting in a warehouse over the weekend), a regular restock window, and a handful of bigger seasonal sales they run year after year. Learn a shop's rhythm and you stop missing its drops by accident.

The usual advice is "follow them on Instagram." That tells you a shop *exists*; it doesn't tell you that this one restocks acros on Wednesday mornings and that one runs flash sales around long weekends. And you can't just memorize a calendar, because the cadence shifts — a shop moves its ship day, adds a sale, goes quiet for a month.

## Auctions, raffles, and waffles.

**Past the fixed-price drops there's a whole second market, and it runs by its own rules.** Hourly auctions that open at a dollar and are won or lost entirely in the last sixty seconds. Raffles that cap entries and draw a winner. And "waffles" — a raffle-auction hybrid the community runs that blends the two.

The mistake newcomers make is treating these like eBay and bidding early. Early bids just set the price for everyone else and bait a war you didn't plan for. The discipline is boring: **decide the absolute most you'll pay for that one coral before it opens, bid that ceiling or nothing, and walk if it goes past.** A dollar-start auction feels like a steal right up until two people who both "have to have it" run it past retail.

## Hunt one coral, not twelve tabs.

**The experienced move isn't refreshing shop pages all day — it's narrowing down.** Keep a short want-list of the specific corals you're actually hunting, each with a rough number you'd be happy to pay, so that when one lists you already know whether to move. Most people lose their grails not in a bidding war but three days late, finding out it dropped from an Instagram story after it had already sold.

It turns "hope I'm looking at the right page at the right minute" into "I'll hear about it when it happens" — which, for a coral that sells out in the first minute, is the whole difference between catching it and reading about it.

## Part B — buy smart and keep it alive.

Winning the race is only half of it. The other half is not overpaying for a name, and not killing the thing in its first month.

## Is it actually that coral?

**This is the anxiety that comes up more than any other:** am I paying grail money for the real thing, or for a common coral wearing an expensive name? With SPS especially, you mostly can't tell. A one-inch brown frag with a few polyps out could be a Walt Disney or could be something that cost the seller twelve dollars — they look the same until both have grown out under the same light for a year.

That gap is exactly what the names exploit. A desirable coral picks up a dozen trade names as it changes hands, and every rename is a fresh chance to mark it back up. **Lineage — a clean chain back to a known mother colony — is the only real defense,** and even that comes down to trusting the seller.

## What it should cost.

**A fair price isn't the lowest sticker you can dig up.** It's what a coral has actually been selling for, across shops, over time. The universal advice — "compare across vendors before you buy" — is correct and nearly useless, because doing it by hand is the part nobody can manage in the ninety seconds a drop gives you.

The coral lists under different names at different shops, the price swings week to week, and a single cheap listing might be a real deal or might be a renamed nobody — a number only means something next to its history.

## Winning it is only half the job.

**Getting the coral into your cart is the part everyone focuses on. The part that actually costs people money is what happens between checkout and the tank.** Three things decide whether the expensive frag lives:

- **The DOA claim has teeth only if you follow it exactly.** A clear *daylight* photo — not a blue-light shot, which hides everything — usually within a short, shop-specific window after delivery, sometimes with the animal still bagged. Plenty of guarantees also carve out "expert-only" or SPS livestock, or won't pay out if you chose a slower shipping option. Read the actual policy before you buy the expensive thing, not after it shows up dead.
- **Pests arrive invisible — AEFW worst of all.** Acro-eating flatworms are nearly impossible to see on a frag, and a standard dip kills the worms but not their eggs. So one dip on arrival isn't enough: dip, watch for eggs over the next couple of weeks, then re-dip. Quarantine anything new for weeks, not days, before it touches your display — a long quarantine is cheap insurance against a problem that can take out a whole colony.
- **Frag size is a survival bet, not just a price one.** A tiny quarter-inch nub has a fraction of the odds a full inch does — a cheap micro-frag of a grail can be money lit on fire. Sometimes the right move is to wait for a bigger cut, or the next drop, instead of gambling on the smallest, cheapest piece.
